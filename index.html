COMPOSITION

The general chemical formula for protein is RCH(NH2)COOH


C is carbon
H is hydrogen
N is nitrogen
O is oxygen
R is the R group


Proteins are made up of smaller molecules called amino acids.

The types of proteins in you body include antibodies, contractile proteins, enzymes, hormonal proteins, structural proteins, storage proteins, and transport proteins

BODY

Antibodies

Antibodies, a Y-shaped protein used by the immune system to identify and neutralize foreign objects such as pathogenic bacteria and viruses, are essential to the body as they. recognize unique molecules of the pathogen, called an antigen, and can block these pathogens by allowing these two structures to bind together, by tagging a microbe or an infected cell for attack, or by neutralizing it directly (for example, by blocking a part of a virus that is essential for its invasion).


Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins that act as biological catalysts. The molecules upon which enzymes may act are called substrates, and the enzyme converts the substrates into different molecules known as products. Almost all metabolic processes in the cell need enzyme catalysis in order to occur at rates fast enough to sustain life.


Messenger Proteins

Messenger proteins, such as some types of hormones, transmit signals to coordinate biological processes between different cells, tissues, and organs.


Structure

Proteins provide structure and support for cells. They also allow the body to move via muscles.


POLARITY

Proteins are both polar and non-polar.


Most proteins have parts that are polar and parts that are non-polar. This is integral to the way they fold. Proteins tended to be in a polar aqueous environment, causing the non-polar parts clump together on the inside leaving the polar parts displayed on the outside to perform catalytic actions. Sometimes the non-polar parts are arranged to span a membrane leaving polar parts at the end on the outside of the membrane to perform some specific task.

PLANTS

Like proteins in general, plant proteins play various enzymatic, structural and functional roles (photosynthesis, biosynthesis, transport, immunity, etc). They also act as storage mediums to meet the growth and nutritional demands of developing seedlings. Proteins perform these functions in their composition and specific structural forms, e.g., through folding, which can range from compact and well-ordered to unfolded and intrinsically disordered.
